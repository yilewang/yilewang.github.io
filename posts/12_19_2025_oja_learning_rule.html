    Oja‚Äôs learning rule

    Published: December 19, 2025

    üìù Idea / Thought

    The invention of the Oja‚Äôs rule is to overcome the problem of unbounded growth when training the neural network with the Hebbian learning rules

    \[y_i = \sum_j w_{ij} x_j\]

    Oja‚Äôs rule

    In Oja‚Äôs rule, it used the multiplicative constrains to re-normalize the weights, so that it will converge to the first principal component. But keep in mind that it‚Äôs only for the one neuron Oja‚Äôs model; If the model has multiple neurons following Oja‚Äôs rule, it will converge to find the subspace of the two eigenvectors, not exactly the two eigenvectors.

    How Oja‚Äôs rule is derived

    we already know the classic Hebbian learning is \(\mathbf{y = w x}\) When updated weight, the learning rule is: \(\mathbf{\Delta w = yx}\)

    • it means that when the presynaptic neurons $\mathbf{x}$ and postsynaptic neurons $\mathbf{y}$ fire together, they will update the weights more. We can also write it in this way: \(\mathbf{w(t+1) = w(t) + \eta yx}\) To avoid unbounded weight growth, for each step, the updated weight is re-normalized by the scalar term itself:

    \(\mathbf{w(t+1) = \Delta w' = \frac{w'}{||w'||}}\) As we already know \(\mathbf{w' = w(t) + \eta yx}\) We can calculate the $\mathbf{||w‚Äô||^2}$ as \(\begin{align} \mathbf{||w'||^2 = (w(t))^2 + 2\eta yxw' + (\eta yx)^2} \\ =\mathbf{||w'||^2 + 2 \eta y^2 + \eta^2 y^2 ||x||^2} \\ =1 + 2\eta y^2 + \eta^2 y^2 ||x||^2 \end{align}\)

    Then, let $\epsilon = 2\eta y^2 + \eta^2 y^2 ||x||^2$ : \(\begin{align} ||w'||^2 = 1+\epsilon, \\ ||w'||= \sqrt{1+\epsilon} \\ = (1+\epsilon)^{\frac{1}{2}} \end{align}\) Then, get the approximate value of $||w‚Äô||$ by Taylor series: \(\begin{align} ||w'||= \sqrt{1+\epsilon} \approx 1 + \frac{1}{2}\epsilon+ O(\eta^2) \approx 1 + \eta y^2 + O(\eta^2) \end{align}\) Let $\delta = \eta y^2 + O(\eta^2)$, and approximate by Taylor series: \(\frac{1}{||w'||} = (1+\delta)^{-1} \approx 1-\delta \approx 1- \eta y^2 - O(\eta^2)\) After we have all elements, we can calculate $\mathbf{w(t+1)}$ now: \(\begin{align} \mathbf{w(t+1) = \frac{w'}{||w'||} = (w(t)+\eta yx) (1-\eta y^2 - O(\eta^2))} \\ \approx \mathbf{w(t)(1-\eta y^2) + \eta yx(1-\eta y^2)} \\ \approx \mathbf{w(t) + \eta yx - \eta y^2 w(t)- \eta^2y^3x} \end{align}\) Finally, we moved $\mathbf{w(t)}$ to the left hand side: \(\begin{align} \mathbf{w(t+1) - w(t) = \eta(yx-y^2w) - \eta^2 y^3x} \\ \end{align}\) The last term will become $O(\eta^2)$, we will have Oja‚Äôs rule: \(\mathbf{\Delta w = \eta(yx - y^2w)}\)

    Here is the multiple neuron version of the Oja‚Äôs learning rule \(\Delta w_{ij} = \alpha(x_j y_i -y_i \sum_{k=1}^m w_{kj}y_k)\)

    Regularization term in Oja‚Äôs rule

    In the last term of the Oja‚Äôs rule, the $\mathbf{y^2w}$ can be $diag(y)^2 w$ or $\mathbf{YY^T}$.

    • diagonal term: All the neurons learn the first PCs
    • off-diagonal term: Neurons learn PC subspace
